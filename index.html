I am a Ph.D. Candidate at Princeton University’s School of Public and International Affairs and a 2025–26 Visiting Scholar in Yale’s Nuclear Security Program. My research interests focus on international security, strategic studies, and foreign policy analysis. I study how states design and implement strategies of coercion, Deterrence and inducement within shifting global contexts.

My current research project, Strategies of Coercion in the Nuclear Era, asks three core questions: When does coercion succeed without war? Which guarantees and institutional commitments move states from brinkmanship to cooperation? And what do alliances and international organizations signal in crises? I argue that credible guarantees and institutionalized cooperation—especially within alliances and UN-shape expectations and can convert coercive threats into compliance. The project combines archival research and declassified materials, original datasets, formal theory, causal inference, and case studies (including the Anglo–American nuclear partnership, North Korea, and Iran).

A second stream of my research examines international organizations and international interventions in statebuilding, fragile governments, and peacebuilding. This work investigates how external actors and action—UN missions, regional organizations, and allied partners—affect governance capacity and patterns of violence, and how their mandates and unity condition local compliance. Conceptually and empirically, this stream complements my core focus on war studies and international security: interventions change the incentives, signals, and constraints that make coercion and deterrence succeed or fail.

Across projects I use mixed methods—archival work in U.S./U.K. repositories, interviews with practitioners, formal modeling, statistical analysis, and survey/experimental evidence. My research has been supported by Princeton SPIA, the Liechtenstein Institute on Self-Determination (LISD), the Center for International Security Studies (CISS), PIIRS, and and Yale University’s Nuclear Security Program at the MacMillan Center for International and Area Studies
